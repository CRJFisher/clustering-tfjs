Linkage & NN-Chain Merge Engine

An O(n²) nearest-neighbor-chain agglomerative engine that merges reciprocal nearest neighbors and updates inter-cluster distances in place via the Lance–Williams recurrence — reproducing scipy/sklearn dendrograms bit-for-bit, tie order included.

Intuition

Hierarchical agglomerative clustering starts with every point as its own cluster and repeatedly merges the two closest clusters until one remains. The naïve way scans every pair of active clusters each round and physically shrinks a distance matrix — overall O(n³).

The nearest-neighbor-chain (NN-chain) algorithm is the shortcut. Instead of hunting globally for the single closest pair every step, you walk a chain of clusters where each one points at its current nearest neighbor. The moment two consecutive chain members are each other's nearest neighbor — a reciprocal pair — you have found a pair that is provably safe to merge right now. Merge them, pop them off the chain, and keep walking from wherever you left off. This is O(n²) time and memory.

Why is a reciprocal pair safe to merge out of strict global order? Because single, complete, average and Ward linkage are all reducible: when you merge clusters i and j into t, t is never closer to any third cluster k than the closer of i and j was. Merging can only push distances outward or leave them flat — it can never pull a third cluster in to undercut the pair you just chose. So a mutual nearest-neighbor pair can never be "stolen" by a future merge.

One shared engine handles all four linkages by swapping a single formula: the Lance–Williams update, which gives the distance from the freshly merged cluster t to every remaining cluster k purely from the old distances D(i,k), D(j,k), D(i,j) and the cluster sizes. The raw data points are never re-scanned after the initial matrix is built. The whole thing operates on a flat Float64Array with active/inactive flags rather than splicing arrays — cache-friendly, and no per-merge O(n²) copies.

The output is a list of merge records (which two clusters, at what distance, the new size) sorted by distance. A downstream pass turns that into the sklearn/scipy children_ tree and distances_, and a union-find replay assigns flat labels when you cut the tree.

How it works

The engine in nn_chain_cluster performs exactly n − 1 merges. Each iteration extends the chain until a reciprocal pair pops out:

  1. Initialize active state. Allocate the flat n×n D (mutated in place), an active flag array (all 1), cluster_sizes (all 1.0), and an Int32Array chain of capacity n with chain_len = 0. linkage.ts:98–106
  2. Seed the chain. When chain_len is 0, scan from index 0 for the first active slot and put it at chain[0]. This deterministic lowest-index seed is part of tie-parity with scipy. linkage.ts:109–117
  3. Walk to a reciprocal nearest neighbor. Let x be the chain tip. Seed the candidate y/min_dist from the previous chain element (if any) using strict-less-than so the previous element wins ties — preventing 2-cycles. Scan all active k ≠ x and adopt k as y only if D[x,k] < min_dist. If the found y equals the previous element, x and y are reciprocal: break. Otherwise push y and repeat with y as the new tip. linkage.ts:119–152
  4. Pop the reciprocal pair. Reduce chain_len by 2 to remove both x and y; the walk resumes from what remains. linkage.ts:155
  5. Choose survivor & removed slot. lower = min(x,y), higher = max(x,y). The higher slot survives; the lower is removed. Capture ni, nj, new_size = ni + nj, set active[removed]=0 and size[removed]=0. linkage.ts:157–167
  6. Lance–Williams update. For every active k ≠ survivor, compute lance_williams(D[removed,k], D[survivor,k], min_dist, ni, nj, size[k], linkage) and write it symmetrically into D[survivor,k] and D[k,survivor]. Here min_dist is D(i,j), the distance the pair merged at. linkage.ts:169–183
  7. Record the merge. Set size[survivor]=new_size and push {cluster_a: lower, cluster_b: higher, distance: min_dist, new_size}. These are active-slot indices, not yet sklearn node ids. linkage.ts:185–192
  8. Loop until tree complete. Repeat until n − 1 merges exist. The chain persists across merges; it is re-seeded only when fully consumed. linkage.ts:108
  9. Sort merges by distance. NN-chain discovers merges in chain order, not distance order; the final ascending sort yields a valid dendrogram order matching scipy/fastcluster. linkage.ts:195
  10. Build children ids (consumer). agglomerative.ts build_children replays merges through a 2n−1 union-find, emitting rows [min(left,right), max(left,right)] with internal ids starting at n. agglomerative.ts:335–365
  11. Cut and label (consumer). Slice merges to n_samples − n_clusters (or filter by distance_threshold), then replay through a second union-find to assign contiguous labels 0..n_clusters−1. agglomerative.ts:158–201

The math

All four linkages are special cases of one update. Merging i and j into t, the distance to a remaining cluster k is:

D(t,k) = αi·D(i,k) + αj·D(j,k) + β·D(i,j) + γ·|D(i,k) − D(j,k)|

The general Lance–Williams form. α, β, γ are coefficients; ni, nj, nk are member counts. Each linkage below picks specific coefficients (the engine writes them directly rather than through this general form).

single    D(t,k) = min( D(i,k), D(j,k) )

The closest pair of members. Equivalent to γ = −½. Produces "chaining" — long straggly clusters.

complete   D(t,k) = max( D(i,k), D(j,k) )

The farthest pair, γ = +½. Favors compact, equal-diameter clusters.

average (UPGMA)   D(t,k) = ( ni·D(i,k) + nj·D(j,k) ) / ( ni + nj )

A size-weighted mean of the two child distances, where ni, nj are the merged clusters' member counts (β = γ = 0).

ward    D(t,k) = √( max( (ni+nk)·t·D(i,k)² + (nj+nk)·t·D(j,k)² − nk·t·D(i,j)² , 0 ) ) ,   t = 1/(ni+nj+nk)

Merges that minimize the increase in within-cluster variance. Crucially the factor t is multiplied into each term separately rather than dividing a summed numerator — this is bit-identical to scipy's _ward, so it flips the very same IEEE-754 ties scipy/sklearn flip. The max(…, 0) guards against tiny negative values from floating-point cancellation before the √.

reciprocal NN:   y = argmink≠x D(x,k)  AND  x = argmink≠y D(y,k)   ⟹   merge(x,y)

A mutual nearest-neighbor pair is the reducibility-guaranteed safe merge.

reducibility:   D(i∪j, k) ≥ min( D(i,k), D(j,k) )   for all k

Merging never makes the union closer to a third cluster than its closest child was. This is exactly why a reciprocal pair found mid-chain can be merged immediately, out of global distance order.

node ids:   leaves ∈ {0..n−1};   merge m (0-indexed) ⟶ internal id (n + m)

build_children maps active-slot merge records to scipy/sklearn children_ node ids: the m-th merge in distance-sorted order becomes node n+m, children stored as [min, max].

Why operation order is load-bearing: the Ward ULP tie-flip

Ward's update is algebraically the same whether you distribute t into each term or sum the numerator and divide once — but in IEEE-754 double precision the two can differ by a single unit in the last place (ULP). On symmetric data, two distances that should tie can end up ordered differently, flipping which pair merges first. This repo uses the per-term form to match scipy exactly. Edge cases & scikit-learn parity

The engine targets exact scipy/sklearn parity, validated by agglomerative_reference.test.ts. Three mechanisms achieve it, and several edge cases keep it honest.

Ward arithmetic ordering is load-bearing. Multiplying t = 1/(ni+nj+nk) into each term separately (linkage.ts:67–75) versus summing then dividing differs by up to one ULP in IEEE-754, which flips exact distance ties and yields a different (still valid) dendrogram. The chosen form is bit-identical to scipy's _ward, so tie order matches scipy/sklearn exactly.
  • Ward sqrt guard. max(…, 0) before √ (linkage.ts:69) absorbs tiny negative numerators from floating-point cancellation on symmetric data, which would otherwise produce NaN.
  • Strict-inequality tie rule. The chain walk uses strict < (linkage.ts:140) and seeds the candidate from the previous chain element (linkage.ts:128–134). On a tie the earlier/previous chain element wins — both preventing infinite 2-cycles and reproducing scipy's strict-inequality NN-chain rule.
  • Deterministic re-seeding. Chain re-seeding always scans for the lowest active index (linkage.ts:110–116), a deterministic choice contributing to reproducible tie behavior.
  • Survivor / removed convention. Survivor is always the higher slot index, removed is the lower (linkage.ts:157–160); records store cluster_a = lower, cluster_b = higher. These are active-slot indices, not sklearn node ids — the build_children union-find converts them.
  • Discovery order is NOT a valid dendrogram order. NN-chain emits merges in chain order; the final sort by distance (linkage.ts:195) is required before the tree can be cut or interpreted, matching scipy/fastcluster.
  • Single sample. n_samples === 1 is handled before the engine runs: labels [0], empty children_/distances_ (agglomerative.ts:148–154).
  • Only reducible linkages. NN-chain is exact only for reducible linkages. Centroid and median linkage are not reducible and are deliberately unsupported (only the four in LinkageCriterion).
  • min_dist is the real merge distance. The min_dist passed as D(i,j) to Lance–Williams is the actual reciprocal-pair distance captured during the walk (linkage.ts:130/140), reused both for the update term and as the recorded merge distance.

Commit history (cb7a7dd, 12adbdf "exact scikit-learn tie parity for ward/complete/average") confirms tie parity is an explicit, tested requirement. Flat labels are derived by replaying the cut merges through a separate union-find with path compression and assigning contiguous labels in first-seen order (agglomerative.ts:169–197).
